Analysis of the Difference in Representation Based on Gender in Movies

Introduction

Nowadays it is hard to imagine our lives without movies. They have the ability to portray a snapshot of reality and show it to people that could never have seen it otherwise. However, within their realities, movies are often criticized for not accurately representing the population. For example, historically, it is often said that women are vastly underrepresented.
The goal of this project is to look at gender diversity in movies over time and see how it influences a movie's success using the CMU Movie Summary Corpus. Not a data scientist? Don't worry! We've added some purple boxes with explanations along the way to help you out.

How many Female Characters are There?

The average fraction of women in the over 40'000 movies in the CMU Movie Summary Corpus is 39%. That sounds reasonably close to 50%, right? However, since the dataset was extracted from English language Wikipedia, movies in English logically make up the majority of it. We took a look at that number in only the movies in the language of Shakespear. We have only 33% of female actors in that group!

What is the CMU Movie Summary Corpus?

The CMU Movie Summary Corpus consists of 42,306 movie plot summaries extracted from the November 2, 2012, dump of English-language Wikipedia. It was then coupled with data from the November 4, 2012, dump of Freebase to add metadata on the movie and information on the actors.

Romance Films for Women, Action Movies for Men

Here we can see that women make up the minority of characters across the genres that have more than 800 actors in them. Most interestingly, of the five most represented genres in our dataset: Drama, Comedy, Romance Film, Thriller and Action women make up more than 40% of the cast in only one: Romance (43.1%). Notice that the fraction of women is much higher in movies that are traditionally targeted at women (like Romance Films) in general when compared to more traditionally masculine genres like Action Movies where the fraction of women is a measly 25.5%.

How are genders portrayed ?

We have seen that men and women are present in different numbers on screen, but how are they described?

First, we will look into the positivity of mentions in the movie summaries. This works by finding the sentences that are associated with men or women and then counting the number of positive, negative or neutral words that are associated with them. Below you can see a part of the movie summary of Cinderella with the male sentences highlighted in blue and the female sentences highlighted in pink. The words that are marked in blue/pink are the ones that were used to determine whether a sentence is male or female. If both are present the sentence is classified as neutral. Before running sentiment analysis the summaries were tokenized the stopwords were removed and everything was lemmatized.

What are Tokenization, Stopword Removal and Lemmatization?

When a text is tokenized it is split into individual tokens (≈words). In stopword removal all of the small words that do not add much information like “is” or “a” are removed. Lemmatization maps different versions of the same normalized words. For example, talking and talked would both become talk.

Cool story bro, but how will this make me more successful?

To answer this question we will first have to take a look at how to define success. We decided to take both the revenue of the movie as well as the average rating of users into account. As suggested by the correlation of 0.23 the link between revenue and rating or budget and rating is very unclear, and the definition of success is thus very important. In the following we defined it as the revenue first, and then as the average rating. When looking at the median box office revenue of two groups, the first group (blue) representing movies with a low percentage of women and the treated group that contains movies with a percentage of women higher than average, we first observe that movies with less women than average seem to be more successful.

What's matching?

We all know not all movies are the same and as we saw earlier a lot of factors could be influencing our result. To account for this, matching takes movies that are as similar as possible to each other except for the variable of interest (which is gender ratio here) and pairs them up so they can be compared to each other.

However, when performing a matching study where movies were matched between these two groups based on release year, budget and movie genres, we realize that there is an opposite trend: movies with more women than the average seem to be more successful! However the difference is not statistically significant, so we cannot conclude from these results whether one group is more successful than the other.

End

When we go through the same procedure with the rating as the measure of success we observe a similar result. This time, there is statistically significant difference, but the effect size is small. People seem to rate movies slightly higher if they have more gender diversity, however, this does not translate to revenue giving Hollywood very little incentive to change anything.

Outlook

We've seen that male and female actors are represented in very different numbers in movies and that there are vast differences across the genres. We've also seen that men are described more negatively and that women are mostly talked about in relation to their relationships or reproduction. Finally, we saw that more diverse movies don't necessarily make more money but might be rated higher among viewers. Below there's a fun little toy to predict the revenue of a film! You can evaluate your skills by a score between -1 and 1.

Example: Da Vinci Code, a 2006 American Thriller, with a budget of 125 million dollars, we suppose a medium gender balance (at the mean), and medium skills at 0.5 (the film is great, but a poor adaptation of the original book). The true movie revenue is 757 million dollars.
